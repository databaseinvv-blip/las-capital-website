An investment fund built to deliver capital gain to Unit Holders

The Fund pursues a balance of capital appreciation and stable returns: value investing in brand leaders with growth prospects, growth investing in businesses compounding net profit at double digits, and profit-sharing financing across supply chain, equipment, plantation and machinery arrangements.

Offered exclusively to Sophisticated Investors as defined under Part 1, Schedule 7 of the Capital Markets and Services Act 2007. The Investment Manager, Aureus Strategies PE Sdn Bhd, is regulated by the Securities Commission Malaysia.

Market Outlook

The Financing Escalator

Reading Bursa Malaysia's LEAP Market reform

By the Newsroom  ·  29 May 2026  ·  12 min read
Reference
The full Consultation Paper No. 2/2026 and supporting documents are on Bursa Malaysia's Public Consultation page.
View on Bursa Malaysia ↗
I

What happened

On 18 May 2026, Bursa Malaysia issued Consultation Paper No. 2/2026 — a joint review with the Securities Commission Malaysia proposing amendments to the LEAP Market Listing Requirements, the ACE Market Listing Requirements, and the Rules and Directives of Bursa Malaysia Securities Berhad. Public comments closed on 15 June 2026.

The paper is unusually candid about why. LEAP launched on 25 July 2017 as a fundraising platform for emerging companies, accessible only to Sophisticated Investors.

54
companies raised RM275.8M via initial listings
12
listed by way of introduction
9
migrated to ACE, two more underway
50
remain listed; eight delisted
In nearly nine years · to date

New listings have slowed to a trickle — five in 2025, four in 2024.

Restricting the market to Sophisticated Investors "has naturally resulted in more modest trading activity." That is the diagnosis. The proposals are the treatment.
II

The four structural changes

1 · Retail investors, with limits

The most consequential proposal opens LEAP to retail investors — anyone who does not meet the SC's Sophisticated Investor criteria — subject to prescribed limits.

Two carve-outs matter. Investments by specified shareholders in their own corporation are excluded entirely — a controlling shareholder or founder has direct knowledge of the business, and capping their participation would work against their role in growing it. And pre-listing investments by other existing securities holders are excluded, since those positions arose from relationships that predate the listing.

Compliance runs on a declaratory basis: the retail investor declares to their Participating Organisation that they will comply. If the PO later learns otherwise, it must stop the client from trading except to sell existing holdings. All investors — SI and retail alike — will now sign a risk disclosure statement before trading.

2 · The exit offer disappears

Under the current framework, a LEAP company transferring to ACE must request withdrawal of listing and offer shareholders an exit offer or equivalent mechanism. The logic was sound: LEAP is a qualified market with lighter requirements; ACE is open and more demanding; sophisticated investors deserved a fair way out if they didn't want to migrate.

3 · A seven-year clock

Here is the trade. In exchange for the easier exit, Bursa proposes a new delisting criterion: a LEAP company that fails to apply for transfer to ACE within seven full financial years of admission — or whose transfer application is rejected — will be delisted.

The number is not arbitrary. Of the nine completed transfers, LEAP tenure ranged from roughly 2 years 10 months to 7 years 4 months, with five falling between five and six years.

4 · Share-based adviser fees

MSMEs face high fundraising costs, and listing compounds them — professional fees and compliance expenses, typically payable in cash, at exactly the moment capital is most useful elsewhere. Bursa proposes allowing advisers to take ordinary shares as part of their fee, subject to conditions.

The mechanism does two things at once: preserves the issuer's cash, and — in Bursa's words — better aligns the adviser's interests with the applicant's longer-term development after admission. An adviser holding stock has a reason to still be there in year three.

III

The ECF green channel

Since 2015, ECF issuers have raised RM917.51 million across 445 campaigns. In 2025 alone, RM139.34 million — up 42.4% on 2024's RM97.84 million — driven by larger campaigns above RM3 million, which made up 45% of the total against 34% the year before. There were 413 ECF issuers as at 31 December 2025.

That momentum has had nowhere to go. Bursa proposes a direct pathway: an eligible ECF company may apply to LEAP without an Approved Adviser, if it clears every gate below.

IV

From information memorandum to listing document

Today's information memorandum is meant to be principles-based and lighter than a prospectus. Market perception went the other way — because it carries prospectus-equivalent CMSA liability for false or misleading statements, it gets prepared like one. Cost follows perception.

Bursa proposes re-designating it as a listing document in a prescribed, structured template — Annexure B1.

Some matters currently inside the Approved Adviser's suitability assessment move into disclosure instead — conflicts of interest, systems and resources for LEAP LR compliance, internal controls and risk management — addressed through the listing document, including a board statement confirming adequate internal controls. What stays prescribed in Rule 4.10: corporate governance track record, public interest, and adequacy of disclosures.

There is also a proposed six-month moratorium on pre-listing investors — a VC/PE firm, substantial shareholder, or connected person who is not a specified shareholder — where shares were acquired within twelve months on or before the listing application and below the issue or reference price.

VII

The caveat that matters

These are proposals. Bursa states plainly that they have not been approved by the SC, that they remain open to public comment, and that the final amendments may differ from what was published. The Exchange will submit them to the SC after reviewing feedback and making changes where appropriate.

Nothing here is a rule yet. Anyone telling you otherwise is selling something.

The direction of travel
Easier in. Easier out. Faster graduation.

Both regulators have said so in the same document, and the Capital Market Masterplan 2026–2030 says it again at the level of national policy. The preparation window is open now, while the rules are still being written — a better time to get your house in order than the month everyone else discovers they need to.
